# Expanded Tipping Points: Sources and Rationale

This document catalogues all ~28 tipping points tracked on the Tipping Points dashboard
page, organized by sector. Each entry includes status, evidence, and source citations.

## Sector Emissions Weights

Climate weights represent each sector's approximate share of global GHG emissions,
used to weight tipping points in the optimism score calculation.

| Sector | Weight | Basis |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Electricity & Heat | 0.25 | ~25% of global GHG (IPCC AR6 WGIII Ch.6) |
| Transport (Road) | 0.10 | Road transport ~10% of global GHG |
| Transport (Aviation) | 0.03 | Aviation ~2-3% of global CO2 |
| Transport (Shipping) | 0.03 | Shipping ~2-3% of global CO2 |
| Buildings | 0.06 | Direct building emissions ~6% |
| Industry | 0.21 | Industrial processes & energy ~21% |
| Grid & Storage | 0.04 | Enabler sector (indirect via electricity) |
| Nuclear | 0.03 | Enabler sector (indirect via electricity) |

| Supply Chains | 0.05 | Critical mineral bottleneck risk |
| Fossil Fuels | 0.15 | Cross-cutting demand-side signal |

## 1. Electricity (weight: 0.25)

### 1.1 Solar LCOE below new coal globally
- **Status:** CROSSED
- **Year:** 2020-2023
- **Evidence:** Utility-scale solar LCOE fell to $0.043/kWh in 2024, an 87% decline
  since 2010. IRENA reports 91% of new renewable capacity added in 2024 was cheaper
  than the cheapest fossil fuel alternative in the same market.
- **Sources:** IRENA Renewable Power Generation Costs 2024; Way et al. 2022, Joule

### 1.2 Clean energy investment exceeds fossil fuel investment
- **Status:** CROSSED
- **Year:** 2023
- **Evidence:** Clean energy investment reached $2.15 trillion in 2025 versus ~$1.1
  trillion for fossil fuels, a ratio approaching 2:1. The electricity-specific ratio
  (renewables vs. fossil power) is approximately 10:1.
- **Sources:** IEA World Energy Investment 2025; BloombergNEF

### 1.3 Renewables >50% of new power capacity
- **Status:** CROSSED
- **Year:** 2015+
- **Evidence:** Renewables have dominated new capacity additions for over a decade.
  In 2024, solar alone represented ~70% of all new generating capacity worldwide.
- **Sources:** IRENA 2025; Ember Global Electricity Review 2025

### 1.4 1 TW of solar installed globally
- **Status:** CROSSED
- **Year:** 2023
- **Evidence:** Global solar capacity reached 1,865 GW by end 2024. The first TW
  milestone took approximately 15 years; the second TW is expected in roughly 4 years,
  illustrating exponential deployment.
- **Sources:** IRENA Renewable Energy Statistics 2025

### 1.5 Renewables overtake coal in global electricity generation
- **Status:** CROSSED
- **Year:** H1 2025
- **Evidence:** Ember's Global Electricity Review shows renewables surpassing coal
  as a share of global electricity generation for the first time in H1 2025, driven
  primarily by solar and wind additions.
- **Sources:** Ember Global Electricity Review 2025

### 1.6 Solar + wind exceed 15% of global electricity
- **Status:** CROSSED
- **Year:** 2024
- **Evidence:** Combined solar and wind generation reached approximately 16% of global
  electricity in 2024, up from ~12% in 2022. This is significant because variable
  renewables at >15% begin requiring grid flexibility investments.
- **Sources:** Ember Global Electricity Review 2025

### 1.7 Second TW of solar capacity
- **Status:** APPROACHING
- **Year:** ~2026
- **Evidence:** With current capacity at 1,865 GW and annual additions exceeding
  400 GW, the second TW milestone is expected around mid-2026. This would demonstrate
  continued exponential scaling.
- **Sources:** IRENA 2025; BloombergNEF Solar Outlook

## 2. Transport -- Road (weight: 0.10)

### 2.1 EVs past 5% of new car sales globally
- **Status:** CROSSED
- **Year:** 2021
- **Evidence:** Global EV share reached ~22% in 2024 (17 million vehicles sold),
  well past the 5-10% S-curve inflection point identified by RMI and Nature
  Communications (2025) as triggering irreversible adoption takeoff.
- **Sources:** IEA Global EV Outlook 2025; RMI X-Change: Cars

### 2.2 EVs past 5% in 15+ national markets
- **Status:** CROSSED
- **Year:** 2023
- **Evidence:** Norway (88-97%), China (50%), Sweden (58%), Denmark (56%),
  Netherlands (48%), UK (~30%), EU avg (~21%), US (~10%), and many more have
  crossed the 5% threshold. Per BloombergNEF, 31 countries had crossed 5% by 2024.
- **Sources:** IEA Global EV Outlook 2025; BloombergNEF

### 2.3 EV price parity with ICE without subsidies
- **Status:** APPROACHING
- **Year:** 2025-2028
- **Evidence:** Battery pack prices fell below $100/kWh in China in 2024. A 2025
  Nature Communications study projects unsubsidized purchase price parity for
  medium-sized EVs in most major markets by 2025-2028, depending on segment.
- **Sources:** Nature Communications 2025; BloombergNEF Battery Price Survey

### 2.4 Cascading global EV tipping point (31 countries past 5%)
- **Status:** APPROACHING
- **Year:** Ongoing
- **Evidence:** BloombergNEF data shows 31 countries past the 5% EV sales threshold
  as of 2024. As more countries cross this inflection point, network effects in
  manufacturing scale, charging infrastructure, and policy support create cascading
  acceleration across regions.
- **Sources:** BloombergNEF EVO 2025

### 2.5 Electric trucks past 5% of new sales
- **Status:** NOT_YET
- **Year:** Current: <1%
- **Evidence:** Battery-electric truck sales remain below 1% of global new truck
  sales, though growth is accelerating in China and Europe. Battery cost declines
  cascading from passenger EVs are expected to make electric trucks cost-competitive
  for short/medium haul by 2027-2030.
- **Sources:** IEA Global EV Outlook 2025; ICCT

## 3. Transport -- Aviation (weight: 0.03)

### 3.1 SAF reaches 2% of jet fuel
- **Status:** NOT_YET
- **Year:** Current: ~0.3%
- **Evidence:** Sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) currently constitutes roughly 0.3%
  of global jet fuel consumption. The EU ReFuelEU mandate requires 2% SAF by 2025
  and 6% by 2030. Production capacity is scaling but remains far below targets.
- **Sources:** IATA SAF Dashboard; EU ReFuelEU Aviation regulation

### 3.2 eVTOL aircraft certified for commercial service
- **Status:** NOT_YET
- **Year:** ~2026-2027
- **Evidence:** Multiple eVTOL (electric vertical takeoff and landing) companies
  (Joby, Archer, Lilium) are in advanced certification stages. First commercial
  service is expected 2026-2027, initially for urban air mobility.
- **Sources:** FAA certification timelines; company filings

### 3.3 Electric regional aircraft certified
- **Status:** NOT_YET
- **Year:** Post-2030
- **Evidence:** Electric aircraft for regional routes (<100 passengers) remain in
  development. Battery energy density limitations (~250 Wh/kg current vs. ~800 Wh/kg
  needed) constrain range. Hybrid-electric approaches may come sooner.
- **Sources:** ICAO LTAG report; Heart Aerospace, Wright Electric filings

## 4. Transport -- Shipping (weight: 0.03)

### 4.1 Alternative fuels reach 5% of shipping fuel
- **Status:** NOT_YET
- **Year:** IMO target: 5-10% by 2030
- **Evidence:** Alternative fuels (methanol, ammonia, LNG as transition) currently
  represent a small fraction of total shipping fuel. The IMO 2023 GHG Strategy
  targets 5-10% zero/near-zero GHG fuels by 2030.
- **Sources:** IMO GHG Strategy 2023; DNV Maritime Forecast

### 4.2 300+ methanol-capable vessels on order
- **Status:** APPROACHING
- **Year:** 2024-2025
- **Evidence:** Over 300 methanol-capable vessels are on order globally, signaling
  a significant industry shift. Maersk alone has ordered 25 methanol-powered
  container ships. Methanol is emerging as the leading transition fuel for shipping.
- **Sources:** Global Maritime Forum; DNV Alternative Fuels Insight

## 5. Buildings (weight: 0.06)

### 5.1 Heat pumps outselling gas boilers in leading markets
- **Status:** CROSSED
- **Year:** 2022-2024
- **Evidence:** In the Nordics, heat pumps have 90-97% market share. European
  average reached 28% (past inflection). In the US, heat pumps outsold gas
  furnaces by 30% in 2024.
- **Sources:** EHPA 2025; IEA Heat Pumps Tracking

### 5.2 Induction cooking reaches 10% of global cooking
- **Status:** NOT_YET
- **Year:** Current: ~5%
- **Evidence:** Induction cooking has reached high penetration in some Asian markets
  (Japan, South Korea) but remains below 10% globally. Growth is accelerating in
  Europe and parts of the US following gas stove health concerns.
- **Sources:** IEA Buildings Tracking; industry estimates

## 6. Industry (weight: 0.21)

### 6.1 Green steel (EAF) reaches 40% of global production
- **Status:** NOT_YET
- **Year:** Current: ~28% EAF share
- **Evidence:** Electric arc furnace (EAF) steelmaking currently represents ~28%
  of global production. Reaching 40% would mark a significant shift away from
  blast furnace-basic oxygen furnace (BF-BOF) dominance. Green hydrogen-based
  DRI-EAF projects are in pilot stage (HYBRIT, H2 Green Steel).
- **Sources:** World Steel Association; IEA Iron and Steel Tracking

### 6.2 Industrial heat pumps reaching 200 degrees C
- **Status:** APPROACHING
- **Year:** 2024-2026
- **Evidence:** High-temperature industrial heat pumps capable of reaching 150-200C
  are entering commercial deployment. This is significant because it enables
  electrification of low-to-medium temperature industrial process heat, which
  represents a large share of industrial energy demand.
- **Sources:** IEA Industrial Heat Pumps report; Vattenfall pilot projects

### 6.3 Green hydrogen below $2/kg unsubsidized
- **Status:** NOT_YET
- **Year:** Current: ~$4-6/kg
- **Evidence:** Green hydrogen from electrolysis currently costs $4-6/kg in most
  markets, compared to the ~$2/kg target needed for competitiveness with grey
  hydrogen. Electrolyzer cost reductions and cheap renewable electricity could
  reach this target by 2030 in optimal locations.
- **Sources:** IEA Global Hydrogen Review 2024; IRENA Green Hydrogen Cost Reduction

## 7. Grid & Storage (weight: 0.04)

### 7.1 Grid-scale battery storage doubling year-over-year
- **Status:** CROSSED
- **Year:** 2023-2024
- **Evidence:** Global battery storage additions roughly doubled in both 2023 and
  2024, with cumulative capacity reaching approximately 120 GWh. This growth rate
  is comparable to solar PV around 2010, suggesting battery storage is entering
  its rapid S-curve growth phase.
- **Sources:** BloombergNEF Energy Storage Outlook; IEA Grid-Scale Storage Tracking

### 7.2 Long-duration storage (>8hr) commercially viable
- **Status:** APPROACHING
- **Year:** 2025-2028
- **Evidence:** Multiple LDES technologies (iron-air batteries, compressed air,
  flow batteries, gravity storage) are in demonstration or early commercial stages.
  Form Energy's iron-air battery is targeting commercial deployment. Commercial
  viability at scale remains 2-4 years away.
- **Sources:** LDES Council; Form Energy, ESS Inc. filings

## 8. Nuclear (weight: 0.03)

### 8.1 First SMR connected to grid
- **Status:** APPROACHING
- **Year:** ~2026
- **Evidence:** China's Linglong One (ACP100) small modular reactor at Hainan is
  under construction with grid connection expected around 2026. NuScale in the US
  canceled its first project (UAMPS) but other SMR designs continue development.
  Russia's floating SMR (Akademik Lomonosov) has been operating since 2020 but
  is a barge-mounted design.
- **Sources:** IAEA SMR booklet; World Nuclear Association; China National Nuclear Corp.

## 9. Carbon Removal (weight: 0.05)

### 9.1 Direct air capture below $200/tonne
- **Status:** NOT_YET
- **Year:** Current: ~$400-600/tonne
- **Evidence:** Current DAC costs range from $400-600/tonne CO2 for leading
  technologies (Climeworks, Carbon Engineering/Occidental). The DOE Carbon
  Negative Shot targets $100/tonne. Reaching $200/tonne would likely trigger
  significant scale-up via compliance and voluntary carbon markets.
- **Sources:** DOE Carbon Negative Shot; Climeworks/Occidental public cost estimates

### 9.2 Carbon removal reaches 1 GtCO2/yr
- **Status:** NOT_YET
- **Year:** Current: ~0.04 Mt from DAC
- **Evidence:** Total engineered carbon removal (DAC + BECCS) is in the tens of
  kilotonnes per year. IPCC scenarios consistent with 1.5C require 5-16 GtCO2/yr
  of carbon removal by 2050. The gap between current scale and required scale is
  approximately five orders of magnitude.
- **Sources:** IPCC AR6 WGIII; CDR.fyi tracker

## 10. Supply Chains (weight: 0.05)

### 10.1 Critical mineral supply diversified
- **Status:** NOT_YET
- **Year:** Current: significant concentration
- **Evidence:** China dominates processing of many critical minerals: ~70% of
  lithium refining, ~77% of cobalt refining, ~97% of gallium, ~60% of graphite.
  The IEA and multiple governments have identified supply chain diversification
  as essential for energy security. No single country should control >50% of
  any key mineral for the transition to be considered supply-chain-resilient.
- **Sources:** IEA Critical Minerals Report 2024; USGS; EU Critical Raw Materials Act

## 11. Fossil Fuels (weight: 0.15)

### 11.1 Peak fossil fuel demand reached
- **Status:** CONTESTED
- **Year:** ~2025?
- **Evidence:** IEA projects fossil fuel demand peaks by ~2030 under current
  policies (STEPS). DNV estimates emissions peaked in 2025. Coal demand may
  have already peaked. Oil demand peak depends heavily on EV adoption speed
  and petrochemical feedstock growth. Contested because some analysts project
  continued growth through the 2030s.
- **Sources:** IEA WEO 2025 (STEPS); DNV Energy Transition Outlook 2025; RMI 2025

## Status Definitions

| Status | Definition |
|--------|-----------|
| **CROSSED** | Threshold definitively passed based on data from authoritative sources |
| **APPROACHING** | Strong evidence the threshold will be crossed within 1-3 years; active trajectory |
| **CONTESTED** | Multiple credible analyses disagree on whether/when threshold is crossed |
| **NOT_YET** | Threshold not reached; significant gap remains between current state and target |

## Key Frameworks Referenced

- **RMI X-Change series:** Technology adoption S-curves and the 5-10% tipping point
- **Lenton et al. (Exeter):** Positive tipping points in climate action (2022-2024)
- **Carbon Tracker:** S-curve analysis of fossil fuel demand destruction
- **RethinkX:** Technology disruption frameworks
- **Rogers (1962):** Diffusion of Innovations theory
- **Way et al. 2022 (Joule):** Empirically grounded technology cost forecasts
